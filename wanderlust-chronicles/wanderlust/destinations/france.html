🇫🇷 France

Art, Culture & Gastronomy — The World's Most Visited Country

⭐ 4.9 Rating 📅 Best: April–June, Sep–Oct 💰 $150–$300/day 🛂 Schengen Visa

France is not just a destination — it is a philosophy of living. Where else can you breakfast on a buttery croissant steps from a Renaissance château, and watch the sun sink into a sea of lavender before dinner? This country hosts over 90 million tourists each year and yet every corner still feels like a secret waiting to be discovered.

Paris: Beyond the Postcard

There is a version of Paris that belongs to the world — the Eiffel Tower at golden hour, the Seine reflecting café lights, the impossibly chic locals in Saint-Germain. And then there is the Paris that belongs to those who wander: the covered passages of the 2nd arrondissement, the African markets of Château Rouge, the philosophy bookshops of the Latin Quarter where students argue over espresso until midnight.

Give yourself at least five days in the capital, and resist the urge to tick landmarks. Walk instead. Cross the Pont des Arts. Sit in the Palais Royal gardens. Buy cheese from a fromagerie and eat it in the Tuileries. The Louvre and the Musée d'Orsay are unmissable — the Impressionist collection at the d'Orsay alone is worth the flight — but build your itinerary around neighborhoods as much as monuments.

The Marais is essential: medieval streets containing the Picasso Museum, the Place des Vosges (Paris's oldest planned square), the Jewish quarter on Rue des Rosiers, and some of the city's best falafel. Montmartre offers the Sacré-Cœur basilica and a village-within-a-city atmosphere. The 11th and 20th arrondissements are where Parisians actually go out.

Provence & the French Riviera

When the lavender blooms in July, Provence becomes one of the most intoxicating places on earth. The hilltop villages of the Luberon — Gordes, Roussillon with its ochre cliffs, Les Baux perched above the Alpilles — look like they were painted by Cézanne (who in fact spent most of his life painting them). The Wednesday market at Apt and the Saturday market at Arles are among France's best, stacked with saucisson, tapenade, and lavender honey.

The French Riviera offers a different kind of glamour: the Belle Époque grandeur of Nice, the extraordinary wealth display of Monaco, the modest bohemian calm of Antibes, and the pink flamingos of the Camargue marshes west of Arles. Cap Ferrat and Èze Village are among the most beautiful spots on the Mediterranean coast. The Matisse Museum and Chagall Museum in Nice are world-class.

The Loire Valley: Châteaux Country

The Loire Valley is studded with over 300 châteaux, from the fairy-tale turrets of Chambord (the largest — 440 rooms, 365 chimneys, a double-helix staircase reputedly designed by Leonardo da Vinci) to the intimate perfection of Chenonceau, which spans a river on six arches. Rent a bicycle and cycle the Loire à Vélo trail between them, stopping in the market towns of Amboise and Blois. The valley also produces excellent wines — Sancerre, Pouilly-Fumé, and Vouvray.

Burgundy & the Wine Routes

France's wine country demands a slower pace. In Burgundy, follow the Route des Grands Crus south from Dijon through villages producing some of the world's most revered Pinot Noir — Gevrey-Chambertin, Chambolle-Musigny, Vosne-Romanée. The medieval city of Beaune, with its spectacular Hôtel-Dieu hospice and its lively wine auction, makes the ideal base. Dijon itself is a handsome city with excellent Burgundian cuisine.

French Food: What to Eat & Where

French cuisine is the foundation of the Western cooking tradition, and eating in France remains one of travel's great pleasures. In Paris, start with an early morning café crème and croissant at a zinc-topped bar. Lunch is still serious — a formule menu at a brasserie (entrée, plat, dessert) for €15–22 is common and excellent. Dinner is an event.

Regional specialties to seek: cassoulet in Toulouse, bouillabaisse in Marseille (the real version, with rouille and croutons), choucroute in Alsace, raclette and fondue in the Alps, galettes in Brittany. The best meals in France are often at small restaurants with handwritten menus, no English translation, and a chalkboard wine list.

Insider Tips

  • Book the Louvre, Versailles & Musée d'Orsay weeks ahead online — queues are enormous without pre-booking
  • The Paris Museum Pass covers 50+ museums — excellent value for 3+ days of sightseeing
  • Learn a few French phrases — even "Bonjour, Monsieur/Madame" transforms interactions
  • TGV high-speed trains connect cities faster than flying once airport time is counted
  • Visit in May or September to avoid August peak crowds and higher prices
  • Many restaurants still don't accept American Express — carry Visa/Mastercard
  • Carry cash for markets, village shops, and traditional brasseries
