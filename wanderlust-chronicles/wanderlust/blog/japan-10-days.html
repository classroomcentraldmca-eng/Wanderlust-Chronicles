10 Days in Japan: The Perfect First-Timer's Itinerary

✍️ Alex Mercer📅 October 15, 2025🕐 14 min read📌 Guides
JapanItineraryAsiaFirst Timer

Japan rewards the prepared traveler like no other destination on earth. Its public transport runs to the second; its food culture runs so deep that a single ingredient can carry an entire philosophy; its hospitality tradition (omotenashi) is so total that it fundamentally recalibrates your expectations of service everywhere else you travel afterward.

This itinerary assumes ten full days on the ground, flying into Tokyo and out of Osaka (or Tokyo, if you loop back). It is paced for a first-timer who wants the essential Japan experience without exhausting themselves — plenty of guides try to cram in too much, and you end up seeing everything and experiencing nothing.

Days 1–4: Tokyo

Arrive into Narita or Haneda and take the Narita Express or Keikyu Line directly to your accommodation. Choose a central base — Shinjuku, Shibuya, or Asakusa all work brilliantly, each with a different character. Day one should be gentle. Tokyo is genuinely overwhelming on first contact. Walk your neighborhood. Find the nearest konbini (7-Eleven or FamilyMart) and experience the small miracle of Japanese convenience store food. Have ramen for dinner. Sleep early — jet lag is real and the next nine days are busy.

Day 2: Harajuku, Shibuya & Roppongi

Start at Meiji Shrine, tucked into a genuinely peaceful forest at the heart of Harajuku. Walk through Takeshita Street's youth fashion scene, then find a standing ramen bar for lunch. Spend the afternoon in Shibuya — cross the famous scramble at least once, browse the remaining Tower Records, and ascend Shibuya Sky observation deck as the sun sets over the endless city below.

Day 3: Asakusa & Tokyo Skytree

Senso-ji Temple in Asakusa at dawn, before the tour groups arrive, is a genuinely moving experience — incense smoke curling past the Kaminarimon gate's giant red lantern in near silence. The Nakamise shopping street leads from the gate into the temple grounds past stalls selling ningyo-yaki and kaminari-okoshi rice crackers. Cross the Sumida River to the Skytree for panoramic views, or save the elevation for Shibuya Sky and instead explore Ueno Park's museums and lotus ponds in the afternoon.

Day 4: Day Trip From Tokyo

Pick one, based on your interests:

  • Nikko (2 hours): the ornate Tosho-gu shrine complex set in ancient cedar forest — Japan's most elaborately decorated shrine
  • Kamakura (1 hour): the Great Buddha, Zen temples, and a genuinely lovely coastal town
  • Hakone (1.5 hours): Mt. Fuji views (weather permitting), traditional ryokan hot springs, and the Hakone Open-Air Museum

Days 5–7: Kyoto

Take the Shinkansen bullet train (around 2 hours 15 minutes — book reserved seats if traveling during peak times). Kyoto has a manageable grid layout in its central districts; rent a bicycle for your first full day. Walk Fushimi Inari's thousands of vermilion torii gates starting at 6am, before the crowds descend — this is non-negotiable advice, the difference between a spiritual experience and a crowd-management exercise is entirely about timing. Explore Arashiyama's bamboo grove (also best at dawn), and visit Nijo Castle in the afternoon for its nightingale floors, designed to squeak and warn of intruders. On your third Kyoto day, take a half-day trip to Nara for the sacred deer park and the giant bronze Buddha at Tōdai-ji temple.

Days 8–10: Osaka

Osaka is just 15 minutes from Kyoto by Shinkansen, or under an hour by regular train. It is Japan's food city — louder, cheaper, and more unapologetically carnivorous than genteel Kyoto. The Dotonbori district at night, with its wall-to-wall neon signage and running crab restaurant mascot, is one of Japan's essential street scenes. Try: takoyaki (octopus balls), okonomiyaki (savory pancakes), and kushikatsu (fried skewers). Then try them all again — Osaka runs on a culinary philosophy called kuidaore, roughly "eat until you drop," and after three days here you'll understand exactly why.

Fly home from Kansai International Airport with enough omiyage (souvenir snacks — a genuine Japanese institution) to keep everyone happy until your next trip, which you will already be planning before the plane lands.

Budget Breakdown for 10 Days

  • JR Pass (7-day): approximately ¥50,000 (~$330) — covers most long-distance rail
  • Accommodation: ¥8,000–¥18,000/night for a comfortable business hotel or ryokan
  • Food: ¥3,000–¥5,000/day eating well, including at least one special meal
  • Local transport: ¥1,000–¥1,500/day for subways within cities
