Northern Lights in Iceland: The Complete Chaser's Guide

✍️ Alex Mercer📅 November 8, 2025🕐 9 min read📌 Adventure
IcelandNorthern LightsAdventurePhotography

I have seen the northern lights more than twenty times. I have never once stopped feeling the way I felt the very first time — that the sky itself is alive, that the universe is performing specifically for you, that whatever you believed about the world before this exact moment might need some quiet revision.

When to See the Northern Lights in Iceland

The northern lights are visible in Iceland from September through March — essentially any month with sufficient hours of darkness. The absolute best months are typically February and March, when darkness is still long but the harshest depths of winter have passed, and geomagnetic activity tends to run higher. The summer months, May through August, have near-constant midnight sun and are fundamentally incompatible with aurora viewing — there simply isn't enough dark sky.

Reading the Aurora Forecast

The Icelandic Meteorological Office publishes a daily aurora forecast at vedur.is, and this becomes your bible for the duration of your trip. You need two things simultaneously: sufficient geomagnetic activity (rated 0–9 on the Kp index, where anything above 3 is genuinely promising) and clear skies over your location. Both conditions are required — a Kp reading of 7 under total cloud cover is meaningless, and a clear sky with a Kp of 1 will likely show nothing to the naked eye.

The northern lights are fundamentally uncontrollable. They come when they come. What you can control is where you are when they do.

Where to Go

  • Þingvellir National Park (45 min from Reykjavík): Dark, flat, dramatic — watch from the lakeshore for reflections
  • Akranes Peninsula (45 min): A mountain backdrop, sea views, and reliably dark skies away from city glow
  • Vík í Mýrdal (2.5 hours): The black sand beach at Reynisfjara, with its dramatic basalt sea stacks, makes one of the most striking aurora settings in the country
  • Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon (5 hours): Aurora reflecting in a lagoon of floating icebergs — arguably the single most spectacular aurora location on the entire island

Photographing the Northern Lights

For proper aurora photography you need three things: a camera with manual controls, a wide-angle lens (24mm or wider is ideal), and a sturdy tripod. Starting settings: ISO 800–3200, aperture f/2.8 or as wide as your lens allows, shutter speed 5–15 seconds. Every aurora display is different — fast-moving, bright displays need shorter exposures to capture the structure; faint, slow displays benefit from longer ones. Experiment continuously and review your shots as you go.

And remember: some things are better lived than photographed. On your first strong display, put the camera down for at least five minutes and simply watch. You will not regret it.

What to Pack for Aurora Hunting

  • Thermal base layers, a proper winter coat, insulated waterproof boots
  • A headlamp with a red-light mode (preserves night vision and doesn't disturb other photographers)
  • A thermos of hot chocolate or coffee — you will be standing still in the cold for hours
  • Spare camera batteries — cold drains them dramatically faster than normal
  • Patience. Genuine, deep patience. Some nights deliver nothing. That is the deal you make.

A note on winter driving conditions: this is a sensitive topic in terms of safety — always check road.is before night drives, carry emergency supplies, and never push through conditions that feel unsafe. If you are ever in genuine distress on Iceland's winter roads, dial 112 for emergency services.
