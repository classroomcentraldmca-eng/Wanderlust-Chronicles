🇯🇵 Japan

Ancient Tradition Meets Futuristic Cities

⭐ 4.9 Rating 📅 Best: March–May (cherry blossoms), Oct–Nov 💰 $100–$250/day 🛂 Visa-free (most nationalities)

Japan is a country that defies expectations at every turn. Nowhere else can you stand in a hyper-modern megalopolis humming with neon and possibility, then step onto a train and in two hours be walking a thousand-year-old forested pilgrimage trail in total silence. Japan rewards curious, patient travelers with extraordinary depth — the more you look, the more you find.

Tokyo: The World's Greatest City

Tokyo is simultaneously the largest city on earth and one of the most navigable. Start in Shinjuku — the neon towers, the izakayas tucked into six-seat alleyways, the impossibly efficient train station with 200+ exits. Drift to Shibuya's famous scramble crossing, the boutiques of Omotesando, and the traditional craft shops of Yanaka. Spend an afternoon in Ueno Park, home to five major museums and a pond covered in lotus flowers. Tokyo's food scene is staggering: the city has more Michelin stars than anywhere else in the world, and the best bowl of ramen of your life costs ¥850 at a counter with eight stools.

Kyoto: Japan's Spiritual Heart

Kyoto was Japan's imperial capital for over a thousand years, and it shows. The city contains over 1,600 Buddhist temples and 400 Shinto shrines. Walk the vermilion torii gates of Fushimi Inari at 5am — before the crowds — and you will understand why this country draws so many return visitors. Sit in the gravel garden of Ryōan-ji in silence. Rent a bicycle through Gion, where you might glimpse a maiko hurrying to an appointment. Stay in a traditional ryokan with tatami floors and a private onsen bath for at least one night.

Nara, Osaka & Beyond

Nara, 45 minutes from Kyoto by train, contains the Todai-ji — the world's largest wooden building, housing a colossal bronze Buddha — and over 1,000 sacred deer that bow for crackers. Osaka is Japan's food city and most extrovert city: louder, cheaper, and more carnivorous than its neighbors. Dotonbori district at night is one of Japan's great street scenes. Try takoyaki, okonomiyaki, and kushikatsu. Then try them again.

Rural Japan: The Less-Traveled Path

The Japanese countryside is often overlooked but deeply rewarding. The Nakasendō trail connects Kyoto to Tokyo through post towns barely changed since the Edo period — Magome and Tsumago are particularly atmospheric. The Kumano Kodo pilgrimage trails in Wakayama are UNESCO-listed and hauntingly beautiful. The island of Yakushima, covered in ancient cedar forests over 2,000 years old, reportedly inspired Studio Ghibli's Princess Mononoke.

Practical Advice for First-Time Visitors

  • Purchase a JR Pass before leaving home — it pays for itself with as little as two Shinkansen journeys
  • Download Google Translate with offline Japanese and use the camera mode for menus and signs
  • Cash is essential — many restaurants and smaller establishments still do not accept cards
  • Book cherry blossom season (late March–early April) travel six or more months ahead — it sells out
  • Learn: "sumimasen" (excuse me), "arigatou gozaimasu" (thank you), "ikura desuka" (how much?)
  • A pocket WiFi device or local SIM card is essential for navigation
