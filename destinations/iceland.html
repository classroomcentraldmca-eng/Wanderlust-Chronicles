🇮🇸 Iceland

Land of Fire, Ice & Northern Lights

⭐ 4.8 Rating📅 Jun–Aug (midnight sun), Sep–Mar (aurora)💰 $200–$350/day🛂 Schengen Visa

Iceland is nature on a truly extraordinary scale — a raw volcanic island perched on the edge of the Arctic where geysers erupt on schedule, waterfalls plunge onto black-sand beaches, and the northern lights dance across crystalline winter skies. It is one of the most awe-inspiring places on earth, and no photograph — however beautiful — comes close to the real thing.

The Ring Road: Iceland's Greatest Journey

Route 1 circles the entire island for approximately 1,332 kilometers. Drive it in 7–10 days and you will experience a near-incomprehensible diversity of landscapes: ancient lava fields the color of charcoal, fjords slicing into the interior, basalt sea stacks, ice-blue glaciers calving into lagoons, geothermal steam venting from hillsides, and waterfalls — so many waterfalls. The southern coast alone has Seljalandsfoss (you can walk behind it), Skógafoss, the black sand beach at Reynisfjara, and Jökulsárlón glacier lagoon, where ancient icebergs drift silently toward the sea.

Chasing the Northern Lights

The Aurora Borealis is visible in Iceland from September through March — any month with sufficient darkness. The key variables are geomagnetic activity (measured on the Kp scale, 0–9; anything above 3 produces visible aurora) and clear skies. Both conditions must be met simultaneously. Check the Icelandic Met Office aurora forecast at vedur.is daily. Drive at least 45 minutes from Reykjavík to escape light pollution. Dress for -10°C and wait. When the aurora comes — when green curtains ripple across a sky dense with stars — it rewires something in your brain permanently.

Reykjavík: The World's Northernmost Capital

Reykjavík is a city of 130,000 people with the food scene of a city five times larger. The New Nordic movement took hold here early, and the city's restaurants range from exceptional tasting menus to brilliant hot dog stands (the famous Bæjarins Beztu Pylsur, favored by Bill Clinton and Anthony Bourdain alike). The Hallgrímskirkja church towers above colorful downtown. By night, Reykjavík's bar scene runs until 4am on weekends, fueled by the Icelandic tradition of rúntur — a long evening circuit of bars.

The Highlands: Iceland's Final Frontier

The Icelandic Highlands are accessible only from mid-June to early September when the F-roads (mountain roads) open. The Landmannalaugar area offers multicolored rhyolite mountains and natural hot springs; the Laugavegur trek (55km over 4 days) is one of the world's great hikes. Þórsmörk valley, surrounded by three glaciers, is breathtakingly beautiful. A proper 4WD vehicle is non-negotiable in the highlands.

  • Rent a 4WD vehicle — many roads, especially in the highlands and Westfjords, require it
  • Book Ring Road accommodation months ahead, especially in summer — it sells out fast
  • Check road conditions at road.is before every drive — conditions change hourly
  • Always pack a swimsuit — geothermal pools are everywhere and profoundly relaxing
  • The Blue Lagoon requires advance online booking; it sells out weeks ahead in peak season
  • Weather changes dramatically in minutes — always carry waterproofs and extra warm layers
