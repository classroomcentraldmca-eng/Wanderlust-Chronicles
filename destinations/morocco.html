🇲🇦 Morocco

Spices, Souks & Saharan Desert Sands

⭐ 4.8 Rating📅 March–May, September–November💰 $50–$150/day

Morocco is an assault on the senses — in the best possible way. The medinas of Marrakech and Fez are two of the world's last great medieval urban environments, labyrinths of souks where saffron mingles with leather and rose water. Hours south by road, the Sahara begins — the world's largest hot desert, stretching to infinity, where a camel carries you toward dunes that turn amber and then crimson at sunset.

Marrakech: The Red City

Marrakech is overwhelming, exhausting, beautiful, and utterly irresistible. The Djemaa el-Fna — the main square — transforms through the day: juice sellers and snake charmers in the afternoon, and by evening a vast open-air carnival of storytellers, acrobats, gnawa musicians, and food stalls producing clouds of fragrant smoke. Behind the square, the souks divide by trade — dyers, spice merchants, leatherworkers, metalworkers, carpet weavers — and getting lost is half the point.

Your base in Marrakech should be a riad — a traditional Moroccan courtyard house converted into a guesthouse. The contrast between the chaotic medina streets and the serene, tiled interior of a good riad is one of travel's great experiences. The Bahia Palace, the Saadian Tombs, and the Majorelle Garden (now owned by the Yves Saint Laurent Foundation) are essential.

Fez: The World's Oldest Living City

Fez el-Bali is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and arguably the most complete medieval Islamic city in existence. Founded in the 8th century, it has 9,000 streets — most too narrow for a car — and over 785 mosques. The Chouara tanneries are Morocco's most iconic image: men standing in stone vats of natural dye, pigeon dung, and saffron, with the leather drying in brilliant colors on rooftops above. Watch from a leather shop above for the full aerial view.

The Sahara at Merzouga

The drive from Marrakech to the Sahara takes you over the High Atlas via the dramatic Tizi n'Tichka pass, through the Draa Valley's date palm oases and kasbahs, past the UNESCO-listed mud-brick ksar of Aït Benhaddou (used as a filming location for Game of Thrones and Gladiator). At Merzouga, mount a camel and ride into the Erg Chebbi dune field as the sun sets. Camp in a Berber tent under a sky so dense with stars it seems impossible.

Chefchaouen: The Blue City

Chefchaouen, in the Rif Mountains, is painted in infinite shades of blue — from cobalt to turquoise to periwinkle — and is one of the most photogenic places in Africa. Founded by Moorish and Jewish refugees from Spanish Reconquista, it retains a distinctive character quite different from the imperial cities. Less crowded than Marrakech and considerably cooler in summer.

  • Bargaining is expected and enjoyable in souks — start at 40% of the asking price
  • Hire a licensed guide for your first day in Fez medina — navigation is genuinely difficult
  • Street food is excellent and safe: harira soup, msemen flatbreads, merguez sausages
  • Dress modestly, especially in religious areas — carry a light scarf or sarong
  • Book Sahara desert camps in advance for peak seasons (December–February, March–April)
  • Trains efficiently connect Casablanca, Rabat, Fez, and Marrakech — highly recommended
