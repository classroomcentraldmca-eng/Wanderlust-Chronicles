Machu Picchu: The Complete Visitor's Guide (2025)

✍️ Alex Mercer📅 August 14, 2025🕐 16 min read📌 Guides
PeruMachu PicchuHistoricalAmericas

Machu Picchu has been on your list for years. You have seen the photographs — the citadel emerging from morning mist, terraces cascading toward the Urubamba valley far below, Inca stonework so precise that mortar was never needed. Nothing fully prepares you for the scale and perfection of the real thing.

Booking Your Ticket

Machu Picchu now operates on a strict timed-entry system with approximately 4,500 visitors permitted per day, spread across multiple entry slots. You must book your ticket in advance at the official government website. Do this months ahead for peak season (June–August), or risk finding no availability whatsoever. Tickets specify a particular circuit — Circuit 1, 2, or 3, each covering different sections of the site — and once booked, you cannot switch between them.

Getting There

The classic approach: fly into Cusco, spend two days acclimatizing to the altitude, take a train from Ollantaytambo to Aguas Calientes (also known as Machu Picchu Pueblo), then a shuttle bus climbs the switchback road to the entrance. The train journey through the Sacred Valley and into the dramatic Urubamba gorge is genuinely spectacular in its own right — window seats on the left side (heading toward Machu Picchu) tend to have better views. PeruRail and Inca Rail both operate services on this route; book these well in advance too, as seats sell out alongside the entry tickets.

Altitude Sickness

Cusco sits at 3,400 meters (11,150 feet) — high enough that altitude sickness affects many visitors regardless of physical fitness. Take this seriously: arrive at least two days before any strenuous activity, drink coca tea (widely offered and genuinely helpful for mild symptoms), avoid alcohol on your first two days, and skip strenuous activity until you have acclimatized. Machu Picchu itself sits considerably lower, at 2,430 meters, so the ruins themselves are less of a concern than the days spent in Cusco beforehand.

Nothing fully prepares you for the scale and perfection of the real thing. Machu Picchu will not disappoint.

On the Day

Arrive at the bus queue in Aguas Calientes at least 30 minutes before your scheduled entry time — queues build quickly even with timed tickets. Once inside, options include climbing Huayna Picchu mountain (requires a separate ticket with extremely limited daily numbers — book this months in advance if you want the classic aerial perspective looking down over the citadel) or hiking to the Sun Gate, Inti Punku, a roughly 1.5-hour return walk along a section of the original Inca Trail with extraordinary panoramic views back over the site.

What Nobody Tells You

Afternoon light is frequently better than morning light for photography, contrary to most advice — mist often obscures the site in early morning during shoulder seasons, and burns off by midday. The crowd thins dramatically after 1pm as day-trip groups depart. The agricultural terraces in the southern section of the site are far less visited and wonderfully atmospheric. Bring proper rain gear regardless of season — mist and light rain roll in unpredictably year-round, even in the "dry" season. And strongly consider spending the night in Aguas Calientes rather than attempting a single-day trip from Cusco; you will be glad of it when you watch the sun rise directly over the ruins in near-total solitude the following morning.

Sample 2025 Costs

  • Machu Picchu entry ticket: approximately $45–65 USD depending on circuit
  • Huayna Picchu add-on: approximately $75 USD (books out months ahead)
  • Round-trip train (Ollantaytambo–Aguas Calientes): $70–150 USD depending on class
  • Shuttle bus (Aguas Calientes–site entrance): approximately $24 USD round trip
  • Licensed guide (recommended, sometimes required): $25–50 USD per group
